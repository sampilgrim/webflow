The history and now

Links across the North Atlantic have been built for centuries

In 1866, the British ship Great Eastern succeeded in laying the first permanent telegraph line across the Atlantic Ocean. In 1956, the first transatlantic cable TAT-1 was laid between Canada and Scotland—specifically, between Clarenville (Newfoundland, Canada) and Oban (Scotland), a distance of 3,584 km (2,226 miles).

Those links are nation-building endeavours.

Electric cables are more complex than telecom cables; thankfully we have developed technologies and expertise over the decades. What could have been construed as a pipe dream twenty years ago is now within reach. Several other projects around the world are tackling longer distances and have demonstrated that they are far safer and far cheaper than any other 24/7 carbon free technologies.

The benefits

Built on mutual trust

With the electrification of the transportation and the heating sector well under way, western democracies are in need of green, reliable and secure new sources of carbon free electricity.

An interconnector is built on mutual trust. It links two big load centres and brings benefits to both sides, mainly in term of optimising cleaner and cheaper renewables resources while reinforcing the two grids.

Canada

For Canada, NATO-L is the opportunity to develop the extraordinary green potential of Labrador and the Maritime Provinces (with Hydro and Wind) and foster tomorrow’s industries (data centres, green Hydrogen) with high qualified good paying jobs.

Europe

For NW Europe, it is another step to increase diversification and security of supply with carbon free power at long term stable and reliable conditions.

NATO

For NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organisation): build a physical link between its two legs, strengthening Energy Security amongst its members in a time when Energy is being weaponized by hostile regimes.

Optimised for demand and resources

East – West interconnectors are of a particular value - they harness the rotation of the Earth around the Sun.

Initial studies have shown that the renewable resources (wind, solar) on each side of the ocean are not correlated, allowing the link to become a virtual 24/7 baseload carbon-free corridor. NATO-L will match the brightest sunshine at midday with the peak demand in time zones +/- 5 hours away.

Solar in Europe can power North America’s breakfast and five hours later reverse flow to power Europe’s evening demand peak. Furthermore, initial modelling show that the wind regimes in Eastern Canada are un-correlated to the ones in NW Europe, allowing an optimisation of wind regimes.

The Path

NATO-L is a currently a “vision venture”

The project will take 10-15 years to develop. Significant hurdles need to be cleared. We don’t believe that short term capital can be reasonably deployed at this stage.

The coming years will be dedicated to shape the project, gathers all talents, and obtain the support of all stakeholders (political, regulatory, technical, industrial, and financial).

Because it’s a collective and multi-generational endeavour of people, nations, and institutions for the benefits of all, it makes sense for the journey to start with a foundation.

The foundation will be a Transatlantic forum where we will gather all major stakeholders: Policy makers, Regulators, Academics, Grid Managers, Investors, Tech providers, and Defense organisations.

The foundation will be UK non-profit (akin to a business roundtable) and will welcome all institutions from NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organisation) countries (public and private) that share the vision and want to contribute to the creation of the project.
